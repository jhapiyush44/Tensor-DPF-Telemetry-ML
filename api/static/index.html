Exhaust System
?
Temperature of exhaust gases BEFORE entering the DPF filter. This is the most important regeneration indicator. Below 300°C = soot builds up. Above 550°C = soot starts burning (passive regeneration). Above 600°C = active regeneration.
?
Temperature AFTER the DPF filter. Normally slightly lower than pre-DPF. If post is much lower than pre, it means the DPF is absorbing heat (filtering soot). A small gap means less filtering happening.
?
Mass of exhaust gas flowing through the system per second. Low flow (under 20 g/s) = idle or very light load, not enough energy for regeneration. High flow (over 80 g/s) combined with high temperature = ideal regeneration conditions.
?
Difference between the current exhaust temperature and its rolling average. Positive = temperature is rising (e.g. after acceleration). Negative = cooling down (e.g. after slowing down or idling). Large positive values suggest recent high-load driving.
State Flags
?
Is the DPF currently in an active regeneration cycle? Your dashboard may show a DPF warning or regeneration indicator light. If you're unsure, select 0 (No). An OBD-II scanner can confirm this.

Is the engine running but the vehicle stationary (speed ≈ 0, RPM ≈ 700–900)? Extended idling is the #1 cause of DPF clogging — exhaust temps are too low to burn soot. Set to 1 if you've been sitting still with engine on.

Is the engine under sustained high load? (e.g. towing, driving uphill, accelerating hard). High load = engine_load above 0.70. This is actually good for the DPF — it keeps exhaust temps high enough for regeneration.
Trip Info
?
How long this current trip has been running in minutes. Short trips (under 15 min) are bad for the DPF — the engine never fully warms up and exhaust temps stay too low for regeneration. Enter 0 if just started.
?
Average speed across this entire trip in km/h. This tells the model your driving pattern — a low average (under 30) = mostly stop-and-go city driving. Above 60 = mixed or highway. This significantly impacts soot accumulation rate.
Rolling Averages (Last 30 min behaviour)
?
Average exhaust temperature over the last 10 minutes. If this is much higher than the 60-min average, it means you recently started driving harder. If it's lower, you've been decelerating or idling recently.

Average exhaust temperature over the last 60 minutes. This captures your overall driving style for the trip. Below 350°C average = predominantly cold city driving. Above 450°C = sustained highway or mixed driving. Key indicator for regeneration potential.
